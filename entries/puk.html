Williams (1856) entry
  • (Page 726 仆꜆ Pʻok꜆ (pok3); here normalised to Pʻuk꜆ (puk1))
    • This is more often pronounced pʻok꜆ pʻuk꜆ (puk1) than fú꜅ (fu6), as given on page 61;
    • ꜀tín pʻok꜆ pʻuk꜆   (din1 puk1 顛仆) slipped down;
    • ꜀kéung pʻok꜆ pʻuk꜆ (goeng1 puk1 僵仆) fell down stiff, as if dead.

    (Reading variation. See 仆 fu6.)

Conway's commentary
  • The reading pʻuk꜆ (puk1) is not unattested; 《集韻》 has the reading 普木切, explained 僵也, as an alternative reading of the classical word 仆, to fall prostrate (for which see 仆 fu6).
  • Williams' reading pʻok꜆ (pok3) is now obsolete, but is interesting to note, as it is an alternative expected reading from 《集韻》普木切.
  • The use of 仆 pʻuk꜆ (puk1) in 仆街 (see below) leads to the perception that the reading is vernacular, even vulgar.
Conway's supplementary entry
  1. To fall prostrate.
Conway's Cantonese entry
  • 【仆街】 (puk1 gaai1)
    1. To fall prostrate in the street. Informally spelt PK.
      • 仆你個街 (puk1 nei5 go3 gaai1): "[May] you fall forward [in the] street."

    Can be used as an optative, an interjection, an adjective, and a noun, varying in meaning from the literal "fall prostrate" to the metaphorical "keel over" or "die" (or one who deserves to do so). For these, see the Wiktionary entry for 仆街.

  • 【仆直】 (puk1 zik6)
    1. To fall flat on one's face, i.e. to fail utterly.
