充 ꜀Chʻung (cung1)

Radical + residual strokes
⼉ + 4
Unicode code point
U+5145
Han unification vigilance
May appear with 𠫓 having 3 strokes (+3) or 4 strokes (+4 assumed here), depending on the font. The two forms are actually separate characters in Kangxi (p.123#10 and p.124#1 respectively). Kangxi considers the first (𠫓 having 3 strokes) to be orthodox.
Alternative forms
  • 𠑽
Fan Wan entry
  • (Vol. 1 Image 58 充) 滿也塞也實也
Williams (1856) entry
  • (Page 40* ꜀充꜀𠑽)
    • To fill (Kangxi 充: 實之也), to satiate (Kangxi 充: […]大充[…]過飽也), to extend;
    • to carry out, to continue (Kangxi 充: 行也滿也);
    • to act, to fill an office (Kangxi 充: 充當也);
    • to stop up, to stuff (Kangxi 充: 塞也);
    • sufficient (Kangxi 充: 備也);
    • fine (Kangxi 充: 美也), long, high (Kangxi 充: 長也高也);
    • ꜀chʻung ꜂ká fo꜄ (cung1 gaa2 fo3 充假貨) to foist in a poor quality of goods;
    • ꜀chʻung ꜃mún ꜁chʻá ꜁ú (cung1 mun5 caa4 wu4-2 充滿茶壺), fill up the tea-pot;
    • ꜂hau ngoi꜅ ꜀chʻung ꜀kwan (hau2 ngoi6 cung1 gwan1 口外充軍), banishment beyond the frontiers;
    • ꜀chʻung shat꜇ (cung1 sat6 充實) stuff it solid;
    • ꜀chʻung úi꜅ (cung1 wui6-2 充會) to contribute to a society.
Conway's commentary
  • Williams' "long": Better rendered "grown".
  • Williams' 充實: Also "fulfilling" or "rich".
Conway's supplementary entry
  1. To feign.

꜃Chʻung (cung5 陽上)

  • 重

重 ꜃Chʻung (cung5)

Radical + residual strokes
⾥ + 2
Unicode code point
U+91CD
Fan Wan entry
(Not present)
Williams (1856) entry
  • (Page 40* 重꜅ Chung꜅ (zung6); here corrected to ꜃Chʻung (cung5))
    • Heavy, weighty (Kangxi: 毛氏曰凡物不輕而重則上聲);
    • (…);
    • severe, heinous;
    • (…);
    • ꜃yau ꜂kí [꜃chʻung] (jau5 gei2 cung5 有幾重) how heavy is it?
    • (…);
    • [꜃chʻung] ꜂tá (cung5 daa2 重打), to beat severely;
    • (…).
Conway's commentary
  • Williams (following Fan Wan) does not provide separate entries for rising-tone ꜃chʻung (cung5) and departing-tone chung꜅ (zung6), whose distinction is still well preserved in modern Cantonese.
  • I have split things up accordingly, noting that the "severe, heinous" sense is able to fit both readings (and, in particular, is rising in 重病, 重刑, etc.).
  • Kangxi has commentary claiming that the rising and departing readings were anciently interchangeable, and that maintaining a distinction between them is 拘泥之過 an "erroneous clinging" on the part of Sung Dynasty scholars:
    按說文柱用切厚也卽與輕重義同集韻柱勇切愼也卽與鄭重義同上去雖有二音𠀤無二義古人三聲通用必謂上去異訓不可通押此宋人拘泥之過也
    However, both Zhengzhang and Baxter–Sagart reconstruct three readings for 重 in Old Chinese, corresponding to Middle Chinese level, rising, and departing.
